FUN FACTS ABOUT SHARKS (didn't use chat GPT...)

Sharks have been around for a long time, with evidence suggesting they've existed for more than 400 million years, making them older than dinosaurs. old geezer yikes!!!
Sharks come in all sizes, from the tiny dwarf lanternshark, which is about the size of a human hand, to the massive whale shark, which can reach lengths of over 40 feet (12 meters), making it the largest fish in the sea.
Sharks have highly developed senses. They can detect electrical fields generated by other animals, sense movement in the water, and have an excellent sense of smell. Some species can even detect prey from several miles away.
Most sharks need to keep swimming to breathe. They have a system of moving water over their gills called "ram ventilation." However, some species, like nurse sharks, can actively pump water over their gills while stationary
Sharks are apex predators and play a crucial role in maintaining the balance of marine ecosystems. While some species are specialized feeders, such as the whale shark, which primarily consumes plankton, others have a more varied diet, including fish, seals, and even other sharks.
Sharks are apex predators and play a crucial role in maintaining the balance of marine ecosystems. While some species are specialized feeders, such as the whale shark, which primarily consumes plankton, others have a more varied diet, including fish, seals, and even other sharks.
Sharks have rows of teeth that are continuously replaced throughout their lives. Some species can shed thousands of teeth in their lifetime. Additionally, shark teeth vary in shape and size depending on the species and their dietary preferences.
Many shark species undertake long-distance migrations, traveling thousands of miles across oceans. These migrations are often linked to feeding, breeding, or seasonal changes in water temperature.
Sharks have evolved numerous adaptations to survive in their environments. These include specialized body shapes for different swimming styles, camouflage patterns for hunting or avoiding predators, and unique reproductive strategies.
Despite their fearsome reputation, sharks are not as dangerous to humans as often portrayed. In fact, humans pose a much greater threat to sharks through activities such as overfishing, bycatch, and habitat destruction. Many species of sharks are currently threatened or endangered due to human activities.
